\begin{abstract}
Fuzzers are often guided by coverage, making the estimation of maximum possible
coverage a key concern in fuzzing.
However, achieving 100\% coverage is impossible for most real-world software
systems, regardless of the time spent fuzzing.
While static analysis of reachability can provide an upper bound, it is often
highly inaccurate.

Recently, statistical estimation techniques inspired by the use of species
richness estimators in biostatistics, where estimating remaining coverage is
analogous to estimating unseen species,
have been proposed as a potential solution.
However, a lack of reliable benchmarks with labeled ground truth has limited a
thorough evaluation of the accuracy of this approach.
Unfortunately, producing large-scale benchmarks with labeled ground truth is
practically infeasible.

To address this challenge, we propose an alternative evaluation criterion for
species richness based estimators.
We argue that, when using species richness estimators, the estimated reachable coverage should remain consistent regardless of the sampling unit used.

To resolve this issue, in this paper, we propose an alternative evaluation criteria
We argue that, when using species richness estimators, estimates of reachable
coverage should remain consistent regardless of the sampling unit used.

We propose to empirically evaluate the species estimation criteria under various
sampling units.

\end{abstract}
